The Themis Project — Phase I

The Problem

Why your AI system fails in ways you can't patch

In five years, you've achieved what seemed impossible: models that pass professional exams, assistants handling millions of queries, code that ships to production. You trained on the entire internet. You scaled to hundreds of billions of parameters.

And still — your system is confidently wrong 15-25% of the time.

Your users have noticed. Your enterprise customers are asking hard questions. Your safety team is bolting on guardrails faster than you can ship features.

The Failures Have Names

Failure What Happens Why It Matters
Hallucination System asserts falsehoods with high confidence Users can't trust outputs without verification
Semantic drift Meaning shifts unpredictably across long contexts Multi-turn conversations become unreliable

Every major lab has published on these. Five years of scaling. Billions in compute. The problems remain.

The Diagnosis

The Hard Truth

These aren't bugs. They're architecture. Your system operates on a single axis: Input tokens → Statistical prediction → Output tokens. Pattern matching at scale. Extraordinarily powerful for generating plausible text. Structurally incapable of generating valid text.

The system cannot:

  • Know whether its output is true
  • Distinguish inference from association
  • Recognize when it doesn't know
  • Maintain meaning across context
  • Defer when it should

Because:

The architecture doesn't represent these capabilities. You can't patch your way to validity. You can't prompt-engineer your way to grounding. The capacity isn't missing from the training data — it's missing from the structure.

What Validity Actually Requires

A claim is valid when it satisfies six constraints — not five, not seven, exactly six:

1

Referential — WHAT is being claimed?

Without it: vague assertions, shifting targets

2

Contextual — Under what CONDITIONS?

Without it: overgeneralization, false universals

3

Premissive — On what GROUNDS?

Without it: unwarranted confidence, no justification

4

Inferential — WHY does this follow?

Without it: logical gaps, non-sequiturs

5

Constraining — What are the LIMITS?

Without it: overclaiming, no boundaries

6

Teleological — What is this FOR?

Without it: pointless precision, missing purpose

Miss any one constraint and the claim is incomplete. It might sound right. It might even be right. But you can't know it's right — and neither can your system. Current architectures check zero of these explicitly.

The Geometry of the Problem

Six constraints is not arbitrary. It's the minimum for structural closure.

A tetrahedron — the simplest three-dimensional enclosure — has four vertices and six edges. Remove any edge and it no longer bounds a volume. It's no longer closed.

Semantic validity works the same way:

  • 4 vertices — the components (claimant, subject, grounds, purpose)
  • 6 edges — the constraints binding them

Remove any constraint and meaning leaks out — the claim can be true in ways you didn't intend, false in ways you can't detect.

The tetrahedron: 4 components, 6 constraints, semantic closure

This structure was discovered 2,400 years ago. Forgotten by modern ML. Recovered here.
